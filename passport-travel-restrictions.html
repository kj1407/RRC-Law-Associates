SEIZING PASSPORTS AND RESTRICTING INTERNATIONAL TRAVEL IN INDIA: LEGAL LIMITS, CONSTITUTIONAL SAFEGUARDS, CASE LAWS, AND IMMEDIATE REMEDIES

June 11, 2026 9 Min Read RRC Legal Desk Team

Introduction

A passport is more than a travel document—it is an important instrument of personal liberty, enabling individuals to pursue employment, education, business opportunities, medical treatment, and family commitments abroad. When authorities seize, impound, revoke, or otherwise restrict a passport or related travel documents, the consequences can be significant. Such restrictions often arise in criminal investigations, economic offence cases, immigration disputes, and national security matters.

This article examines the constitutional and statutory framework governing travel restrictions in India, including the Passports Act, 1967, key judicial precedents, notable real-world cases, and the legal remedies available to affected individuals.

Constitutional Foundation: The Right to Travel Abroad

The right to travel abroad has long been recognized by Indian courts as an important facet of personal liberty protected under Article 21 of the Constitution of India.

Satwant Singh Sawhney v. Assistant Passport Officer (1967)

In this landmark judgment, the Supreme Court recognized that the right to travel abroad forms part of personal liberty. The Court held that such liberty cannot be curtailed except through a valid procedure established by law. This decision laid the constitutional foundation for later developments concerning passport rights and travel freedoms.

Maneka Gandhi v. Union of India (1978)

The most influential judgment in this area arose when the Government impounded the passport of Maneka Gandhi in the public interest. The Supreme Court held that:

  • The right to travel abroad is part of personal liberty under Article 21.
  • Any restriction must be supported by law.
  • The procedure adopted must be fair, just, and reasonable.
  • Executive discretion cannot be arbitrary.

The judgment transformed Indian constitutional law by establishing that any action affecting personal liberty must satisfy principles of fairness, reasonableness, and due process. Even today, Maneka Gandhi remains the leading authority on passport restrictions and personal liberty.

Can Police Seize a Passport?

Many individuals assume that investigating agencies have unrestricted power to confiscate passports during criminal investigations. The legal position is considerably more nuanced. The Supreme Court has clarified that police authorities may seize a passport during an investigation where circumstances warrant such action. However, they do not possess the statutory authority to permanently impound the document.

Once seized, the passport must ordinarily be forwarded to the competent Passport Authority, which alone possesses the statutory power under the Passports Act, 1967 to determine whether the passport should be impounded or revoked.

Police Can:

  • Temporarily seize a passport during investigation.
  • Produce the passport before competent authorities.
  • Use the document as evidence where legally permissible.

Police Cannot:

  • Permanently impound a passport.
  • Exercise powers reserved for Passport Authorities.
  • Indefinitely restrict foreign travel without lawful authority.

Passport Seizure vs Passport Impounding: A Crucial Legal Distinction

The terms “seizure” and “impounding” are often used interchangeably, but they carry distinct legal meanings.

Classification Legal Core Scope Governing Ruling Guidance
Passport Seizure Refers to the temporary taking of possession of a passport during an investigation or legal proceeding. Suresh Nanda v. CBI (2008):
Investigating agencies may seize, but the authority to impound rests solely with the Passport Authority.
Passport Impounding A statutory action that restricts or suspends the use of a passport and effectively prevents international travel.

This distinction is critical because unlawful attempts to bypass statutory procedures can be challenged before courts.

Can Courts Restrict International Travel?

Courts possess the authority to impose reasonable conditions in criminal proceedings to secure the presence of an accused person. However, judicial powers must be exercised consistently with statutory provisions.

Recent judicial pronouncements have reiterated that courts cannot indirectly exercise powers that effectively amount to passport impounding where such authority is specifically vested in Passport Authorities under the Passports Act. The guiding principle remains clear: restrictions on international travel must be lawful, proportionate, and supported by statutory authority.

Beyond Passports: Other Travel Documents That May Be Restricted

Travel restrictions are not limited to passports alone. Several other travel-related documents and mechanisms may affect an individual's ability to travel internationally.

1. Visa Cancellation

A valid passport does not guarantee international travel. Foreign governments may revoke or cancel visas on grounds such as criminal allegations, immigration violations, security concerns, or fraudulent applications.

2. Overseas Citizen of India (OCI) Cards

OCI registration may be cancelled in certain circumstances, including fraudulent procurement, concealment of material facts, or activities prejudicial to India's interests. Cancellation of OCI status can significantly affect travel and residency rights.

3. Emergency Travel Documents

Indian diplomatic missions abroad may issue emergency certificates or temporary travel documents in situations involving lost, stolen, or revoked passports, or urgent repatriation requirements.

4. Look out Circulars (LOCs)

An LOC is a powerful travel-control mechanism available to investigative agencies. It does not cancel a passport; instead, it alerts immigration authorities to intercept, detain, or notify authorities when an individual attempts to travel internationally. LOCs commonly arise in economic offence investigations, money laundering cases, bank fraud matters, corruption proceedings, and national security investigations.

5. Immigration Alerts and Departure Restrictions

Immigration databases may contain alerts that effectively restrict international movement even where a passport remains technically valid.

High-Profile Cases That Captured National Attention

  1. Maneka Gandhi Passport Impounding Case: This case reshaped Indian constitutional jurisprudence and established the modern understanding of personal liberty and procedural fairness.
  2. Nirav Modi and Mehul Choksi: Following the Punjab National Bank fraud investigations, authorities suspended and revoked the passports of Nirav Modi and Mehul Choksi. The case sparked nationwide debate regarding passport cancellation, international extradition, economic offences, and cross-border enforcement mechanisms.
  3. Vijay Mallya: The Vijay Mallya proceedings became one of India's most discussed examples of international enforcement challenges involving alleged economic offences and extradition efforts.
  4. Lalit Modi: Questions surrounding passport status, extradition, and international travel restrictions generated extensive public and legal debate.
  5. Zakir Naik: Proceedings involving the passport status of Zakir Naik highlighted the intersection of national security concerns, criminal investigations, and travel restrictions.

When Can a Passport Be Impounded?

Under the Passports Act, 1967, a passport may be impounded or revoked in circumstances involving:

  • National security concerns.
  • Sovereignty and integrity of India.
  • Pending criminal proceedings.
  • Fraudulent procurement of passport.
  • Court-directed action.
  • Public interest considerations recognized by law.

However, such decisions remain subject to judicial review and constitutional scrutiny. Arbitrary restrictions may be challenged before competent courts.

What Should You Do If Your Passport Is Seized?

The first few days following seizure are often crucial. Follow these immediate procedural steps:

1
Obtain All Relevant Documents

Request physical copies of the seizure memo, written orders, court directions, and communications issued by authorities. Never rely solely on oral instructions.

2
Identify the Authority Holding the Passport

Determine whether the passport is currently with police authorities, the Passport Authority, a criminal court, CBI, Enforcement Directorate (ED), or the SFIO. The appropriate legal remedy depends on who exercises control.

3
Seek Legal Advice Immediately

Prompt legal intervention may prevent employment losses, immigration complications, business disruption, and long-term travel restrictions.

4
Challenge Unlawful Restrictions

Available remedies may include representations before Passport Authorities, applications before criminal courts, writ petitions before High Courts, and constitutional challenges under Articles 14 and 21.

5
Apply for Urgent Travel Permission

Courts may grant temporary permission for travel involving employment opportunities, medical treatment, educational programmes, family emergencies, or business obligations.

Legal Remedies Available

Individuals affected by passport seizure, impounding, revocation, LOCs, or travel restrictions may have access to several remedies:

Administrative Remedies

  • Representation before Passport Authorities.
  • Statutory appeals.
  • Review applications.
  • Requests for reconsideration.

Judicial Remedies

  • Writ Petitions under Article 226.
  • Constitutional challenges under Articles 14 and 21.
  • Applications seeking release of passport.
  • Petitions challenging LOCs.
  • Requests for temporary travel permission.

Conclusion

The power to restrict international travel is significant and cannot be exercised arbitrarily. Indian constitutional jurisprudence firmly recognizes that the right to travel abroad forms an integral part of personal liberty.

Whether the issue involves passport seizure, impounding, visa cancellation, OCI cancellation, or a Look out Circular, authorities must act within the framework of law and constitutional safeguards. For individuals facing travel restrictions, understanding the distinction between lawful regulation and unlawful interference is essential. Equally important is obtaining prompt legal advice to protect fundamental rights and pursue effective remedies before the appropriate forum.
