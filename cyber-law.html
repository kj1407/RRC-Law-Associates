Cyber Law, Data Protection & Digital Evidence

A Comprehensive Legal Framework for Cyber Crime Defense, Data Privacy Compliance, Digital Forensic Audits, and Electronic Evidence Admissibility under Indian IT Laws.

The rapid evolution of digital infrastructure has brought forth complex legal challenges involving data breaches, online financial fraud, and digital defamation. In the modern courtroom, the integrity of an electronic record—be it an email, a WhatsApp thread, or a server log—often dictates the outcome of both civil and criminal litigation. Understanding the intersection of the Information Technology Act and the new Bharatiya Sakshya Adhiniyam (BSA) is essential for securing digital assets and reputation.

We manage the complete lifecycle of a cyber matter, from initial forensic coordination to high-court defense and regulatory compliance audits.

Frequently Asked Questions

Under the Digital Personal Data Protection (DPDP) Act, any business entity determining the purpose and means of processing personal data is classified as a Data Fiduciary. Baseline liabilities include implementing robust technical and organizational security safeguards to prevent data breaches, deploying clear and explicit itemized consent notices available in scheduled languages, appointing a Data Protection Officer (DPO), and ensuring immediate erasure of personal data once the specified processing purpose is fulfilled.

Section 63 of the Bharatiya Sakshya Adhiniyam (BSA) replaces the legacy provisions of Section 65B of the Evidence Act for authenticating electronic records. For digital evidence (such as emails, system logs, or WhatsApp chat trails) to be admissible in court, it must be accompanied by a specific statutory certificate. This certificate must formally verify the source device's operating condition, preserve cryptographic hash values, and be signed by both the person in lawful command of the device and an expert witness to validate system data integrity.

As mandated by the **Indian Computer Emergency Response Team (CERT-In)** cyber security directives, all corporate entities, service providers, and data centers are legally obligated to report specified cyber security incidents—including ransomware attacks, data breaches, and system defacements—**within 6 hours** of identifying or being notified of the breach. Compliance tracking requires swift legal-technical forensic synchronization to avoid heavy administrative and statutory penalties.

Immediate mitigation demands a rapid three-tier approach: filing a formal cyber crime complaint to trigger financial transaction freezes, initiating statutory claims before the **Adjudicating Officer under the Information Technology Act, 2000** for data compensation damages, and filing parallel criminal actions under unified penal codes for impersonation and digital forgery. Concurrently, our desk structures protective liability actions to preserve the brand’s contractual integrations and commercial standing from vendor litigation loops.
